# The Insurance Claim Lifecycle

## Training Module for 1-800-Packouts SDR Onboarding

This document explains how insurance claims work from start to finish. Every conversation an SDR has — with homeowners, adjusters, general contractors, or property managers — revolves around this process. Understanding the flow is essential for asking the right discovery questions and knowing where a lead is in their journey.

---

## Step 1: The Loss

Something bad happens to a home:
- Structure fire — this is the primary lead source through fireleads.com
- Water damage — burst pipe, supply line failure, water heater failure, flooded basement
- Smoke damage — can occur even without visible fire
- Storm damage — less common in Arizona but it happens

This event starts everything. For fire leads, the company often knows about it within hours thanks to fire department dispatch monitoring.

## Step 2: Claim Filed

The homeowner calls their insurance company to report the loss. This creates a claim number. The homeowner might not have done this yet when the SDR calls — that is okay. One of the discovery questions is "Has your insurance company been in touch yet?"

Common insurance carriers:
- State Farm, Allstate, USAA, Travelers, Liberty Mutual, Farmers, American Family
- Regional carriers specific to Arizona

Knowing the carrier matters because some are easier to work with than others, and some maintain preferred vendor lists.

## Step 3: Adjuster Assigned

The insurance company assigns an adjuster to the claim. The adjuster:
- Inspects the damage
- Determines what is covered
- Approves vendors (like 1-800-Packouts)
- Writes the scope of work (what needs to be done)

Adjusters are gatekeepers. If an adjuster knows and trusts 1-800-Packouts, they will recommend us to the homeowner. Building adjuster relationships is a long game but extremely valuable.

Types of adjusters:
- Staff adjuster: Full-time employee of the insurance carrier
- Independent adjuster: Contractor who handles claims for multiple carriers
- Public adjuster: Hired by the homeowner, not the carrier — less common

## Step 4: Scope Written

The adjuster writes a scope of work using Xactimate — the industry-standard estimating software. This document lists every line item that insurance will pay for: packing labor hours, number of boxes, storage months, cleaning processes, transportation, and more.

Each line item has a CPS code (like a product code) and a unit price set by Xactimate's pricing database, which is updated monthly based on local market rates.

Key terms:
- RCV (Replacement Cost Value): What it would cost to replace an item brand new. This is the big number.
- ACV (Actual Cash Value): RCV minus depreciation. This is what insurance initially pays.
- Depreciation: The reduction in value because an item is old or used. Insurance holds this back initially, then releases it when the work is completed.
- Supplement: An additional scope request when the original estimate did not cover everything. Very common — almost every job gets supplemented.

The SDR will never write a scope and will never discuss pricing. But understanding this process allows intelligent conversation with adjusters and general contractors.

## Step 5: Vendor Selected

The homeowner (or their contractor or adjuster) chooses a packout vendor. How 1-800-Packouts gets chosen:
- Direct from homeowner: They received a call from a fire lead, liked the company, and told their adjuster they want 1-800-Packouts
- Referral from GC or restoration company: A general contractor with an existing relationship subcontracts the packout
- Referral from adjuster: The adjuster recommends us because we are on their preferred vendor list
- Referral from property manager: A PM has a water loss at a rental unit and calls their go-to packout company

The SDR's job is to open ALL of these channels. That is why there are 4 different call scripts for 4 different lead types.

## Step 6: Work Authorization

Before work can begin, a work authorization is needed — a signed agreement giving permission to enter the home, pack contents, and store them. Insurance has approved the scope. Matt the owner handles all work authorizations.

The SDR's role stops at getting the lead to this point. The SDR discovers, qualifies, and escalates. Matt closes.

## The Timeline

From loss to pack-back, a typical job takes:
- Loss to first call: Hours (fire leads) to days (cold outreach)
- Claim filed to adjuster assigned: 1 to 5 days
- Adjuster visit to scope written: 1 to 2 weeks
- Scope to work authorization: Days to weeks (depends on homeowner decisions)
- Packout itself: 1 to 3 days for a typical home
- Storage: 3 to 12 months (sometimes longer)
- Pack-back: 1 to 2 days

The whole cycle can take 6 to 18 months. That is why follow-up cadence matters — a lead from today might not need services for weeks, but the SDR needs to stay top of mind.

## Key Takeaways

1. Every conversation revolves around where the lead is in the 6-step claim process
2. Adjusters are gatekeepers — building relationships with them is high-value, long-cycle work
3. The SDR discovers, qualifies, and escalates. Matt closes.
4. Follow-up cadence matters because the cycle is long
5. Never discuss pricing or write scopes — understand the process so you can speak intelligently
